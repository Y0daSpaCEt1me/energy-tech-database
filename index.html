Nuclear Isomer Triggering (NIT)

Early Research
Click to expand

How It Works

Nuclear isomers are excited states of atomic nuclei that can store energy for extended periods. When triggered to release this energy through specific gamma ray frequencies, they emit concentrated energy bursts.

Key Materials

  • Hafnium-178m2 (primary isomer candidate)
  • Tantalum-180m
  • Specialized gamma ray sources

Research Groups

University of Texas at Dallas - AMO Physics Lab Investigating ultrafast isomerization processes
Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory Studying energy release mechanisms and triggering reliability

Current Problems

  • Extremely difficult to trigger reliably (controversial results)
  • High costs for isomer production
  • Energy input vs output efficiency unclear
  • Scaling challenges from laboratory to practical application

Energy Output & Applications

Theoretical: Very high energy density storage (~MJ/g)
Potential Uses: Energy storage, compact power sources, specialized applications

Benefits vs Other Tech

  • Extremely high energy density
  • Long-term energy storage capability
  • Clean energy release (no combustion)

Molten Salt Reactors (MSR)

Active Development
Click to expand

How It Works

Nuclear fuel is dissolved in a molten fluoride salt that acts as both fuel and coolant. The liquid fuel circulates through the reactor core, enabling continuous operation and inherent safety features.

Key Materials

  • Fluoride salts (LiF-BeF2, FLiBe)
  • Thorium-232 and/or Uranium-233
  • Graphite moderator
  • Hastelloy-N or similar corrosion-resistant alloys

Research Groups

ThorCon Power Developing commercial pilot reactor in Indonesia
Terrestrial Energy Creating modular IMSR design for near-term deployment
Copenhagen Atomics Working on compact wasteburner design
Chinese Academy of Sciences Operating experimental TMSR research program

Current Problems

  • Materials corrosion from hot molten salts
  • Regulatory approval challenges (no commercial precedent)
  • High initial capital costs
  • Salt chemistry management complexity

Energy Output & Applications

Output: 50-500 MWe (depending on design)
Uses: Base-load electricity generation, industrial process heat, hydrogen production

Benefits vs Other Tech

  • Inherent safety (passive shutdown mechanisms)
  • Can consume nuclear waste
  • Higher thermal efficiency than solid-fuel reactors
  • Reduced pressure requirements (atmospheric operation)

Molecular Solar-Thermal (MOST)

Research Phase
Click to expand

How It Works

Uses photoswitchable molecules that absorb solar energy and change molecular structure, storing energy in chemical bonds. Heat or catalysts trigger the reverse reaction, releasing stored energy on demand.

Key Materials

  • Azobenzene derivatives
  • Norbornadiene-quadricyclane systems
  • Metal catalysts (for energy release)
  • Transparent containers/solar collectors

Research Groups

Chalmers University of Technology Leading research on improving energy density and cycle lifetime
MIT Chemical Engineering Developing new molecular candidates with better properties

Current Problems

  • Low energy storage density (~0.1 MJ/kg)
  • Molecular degradation after repeated cycles
  • Expensive specialized molecules
  • Slow energy release rates
  • Not yet viable at commercial scale

Energy Output & Applications

Output: Low (designed for heat storage, not electricity)
Uses: Building heating, water heating, localized thermal applications

Benefits vs Other Tech

  • Long-term storage without energy loss
  • No moving parts or batteries needed
  • Can be stored and transported as liquid fuel
  • Renewable and potentially recyclable

Anuetronic Fusion

Active Development
Click to expand

How It Works

Anuetronic fusion is fusion where the output energies are exictied protons or Alpha particles instead of nuetrons and their harmful radiation. This is typically done with a proton and Boron or Deuterium and Helium-3. The proton and Boron reaction will lead to 3 alpha particles and 8.68 MeV, which then can be directly turned into energy. For Deuterium (hydrogen isotope with one neutron) and Heluim-3, an alpha particle and proton are released with 18.3 MeV, and this can also be streemlined into energy. Current work maily focuses on proton-Boron reactions using high-intensity lasers and beam target setups. Magnetic confinement, such as field-reversed configurations, dense plasma, and mirror-like traps are other concepts frequently being used. No matter the meathod the goal is the same: to get a hot, dense plasma of the chosen fuel and hold it long enough for many fusion reactions. Because p-B-11 and D-He-3 have such a Coulomb barriers, the systems much have such a high tempature. This is acheived by strong pulshed currents, more common in FRC, that self heats, High power RF or microwave systems, and intense lasers that create short lived plasmas in solid or form targets. Meeting the Lawson criterion (density x tempature x confinement time) is much harder than in a D-T designs, that is the Hydrogen fusion that releases nuetrons. Most of the focus is on manging confienment and losses; specifically paying attention to particle escapses along magnetic field lines, instabilities and turbulence that cuase rapid energy loss, and Bremsstrahlung radiation (X-ray losses from hot electrons). Once acheived, the power can be harvested directly! This can be done by guiding the charged particles out of the core with magnetic fields or by slowing them down in electrostatic fields, leading to dirct conversion. Setting up grids or electrodes at high voltage so energic ions climb a potenial hill and give up kentic energy as electrical potential is one way to collect the energy, the other way is letting charged currents in magnetic structures induce power into coils (inductive conversion). Unfortunately there is no positive net energy, however, nanosecond bursts have been achieved. The benefits of direct energy from the charged particles and less damage makes Anuetronic Fusion an attracktive alternative energy type, as it is currently in active developement in several different labs and private companies.

Key Materials

  • Proton-boron-11
  • Deuterium-helium-3

Research Groups

TAE Technologies Long running company using field-reversed configuration (FRC) plasmas with hydrogen-boron 11 fuel.
Princeton Fusion Systems Compact FRC devices using D-He3 fuel for mobile power and spaceraft propulsion.
HB11 energy Laser-driven internal fusion with proton-boron-11 fuel. Empashizes anuetronic direct electric concepts
Marvel Fusion High power laser-initiated fusion with proton-poron-11 in nanostructed targets. In partnership with Colorado State University
Alpha Ring Microfusion company explicitly focused on a specialized, radiation free proton-boron reaction for distributed clean energy systems.
Helion Energy FRC based, plused devices using D-He-3 fuel with the goal of direct energy conversion.

Current Problems

  • The Coulomb barrier is higher for heavier, more highly charged nuclei lke boron and heluim-3
  • For proton-boron, the confinement parameter nTt is hundreds of times more demanding than for D-T
  • Bremsstrahlung and related radiation losses grow at high tempatures, and can prevent net energy gain
  • Unconventional confinement schemes (dense plasma focus, FRCs, and advanced laser concepts)
  • Not yet viable at commercial scale

Energy Output & Applications

Output: Proton-boron = 8.68 MeV (p+ Boron-11 = 3a +8.68 MeV) Deuterium-heluim-3 = 18.3 MeV (D+He= a + p +18.3MeV)
Uses: Fusion without the harful nuetrons radiation and direct energy converion, which can save a lot of complications.

Benefits vs Other Tech

  • Greatly reduces nuetron radiation, which is better for less material damage and overall waste
  • Instead of a steam cycle, in priciple, direct electric conversion can be used (electrostatic or inductive). This can improve efficiency and plant complexity
